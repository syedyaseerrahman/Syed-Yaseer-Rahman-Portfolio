1. Purpose & Coverage
- We compare five global retailers—Walmart and Costco (US), Alibaba (China), Carrefour (Europe + partial LatAm), and Magazine Luiza (Brazil)—spanning 2019–2024.
- This timeframe captures pre-/post-pandemic changes, e-commerce surges, interest rate hikes, and the shift toward omnichannel retailing.
- We use Altman Z-Scores (structural solvency) plus Merton Distance to Default (market-driven default probability) to see how stable vs. emerging markets shape credit risk.

2. Why These Metrics
- As credit analysts, we look at working capital usage, debt/EBITDA norms, margin stability, and each region’s macro environment.

- Walmart & Costco operate in the US: relatively stable, albeit rising labor costs and fierce competition from Amazon.
- Alibaba contends with China’s regulatory environment (data privacy, fintech oversight) plus geopolitical tensions.

- Both US firms (Walmart/Costco) hold high ratings (AA / A+), aligned with stable macro conditions and strong domestic demand.
- Alibaba: A+ rating partially reflects large scale & liquidity, but local regulatory pressure could diverge from rating agencies’ optimism if those clampdowns worsen.
- Carrefour: BBB rating suggests moderate caution in line with Europe’s maturity.
- Mag. Luiza: BB- rating converges with Brazil’s volatile macro; high interest rates hamper expansions, thus justifying a speculative grade.

1. Profitability & Efficiency
- Alibaba: Remarkably high margins vs. typical low-margin retail. But net margin fell from ~18.5% in 2019 to 13.6% (2024) amid regulatory fines, local competition.

- Carrefour & Mag. Luiza: Both sub-2% net margin. Their lower profitability indicates vulnerability to margin shocks, partly explaining lower ratings (BBB, BB-).

2. DuPont Justifications
- Costco merges high asset turnover (~2.3) with moderate equity multiplier => best ROE in the group, aligning with rating agencies’ positivity.
- Alibaba has strong net margin but a meager asset turnover (0.6). Mismatch yields ~12.8% ROE, reflecting intangible cloud assets.
- Walmart: Balanced synergy of moderate asset turnover (1.4) and scale-based margin. ROE near 17% → stable, supporting AA rating.
- Carrefour: Lower margin + moderate asset turnover (1.2) hamper ROE (~9.5%), consistent with a BBB rating that acknowledges modest returns in a mature market.
- Mag. Luiza’s decent turnover (1.8) is offset by tiny margin (0.6%), weakening returns and credit stance.

1. Synthesis
- Walmart & Costco: Both altman and merton confirm minimal default risk; rating agencies’ high grades (AA, A+) converge with these models.
- Alibaba: Shows a moderate mismatch—A+ vs. a 2.80 altman. Rating agencies emphasize intangible brand value, large liquidity, overshadowing structural signals.
- Carrefour: Converges moderately with BBB; near-zero immediate default from Merton but altman suggests deeper fragility if macro or cost shocks occur.
- Mag. Luiza: Z=1.42, Merton=2.12 → high risk, matching the BB- rating (speculative).

2. Integrating Rating Methodology, ESG, & Macro
- Agencies often prioritize free cash flow or intangible brand equity. For instance, Alibaba’s brand strength helps maintain A+ despite a downward-trending altman.
- ESG factors: Walmart invests in sustainable supply chains; Carrefour has carbon-reduction targets. When effective, ESG can reduce long-term operational risks and stabilize credit profiles.
- Macroeconomic cycles (e.g., 2019–2024 disruptions, US–China tensions, high Brazilian rates) shaped expansions for Mag. Luiza or regulatory pressures for Alibaba. Ratings weigh if these shocks are transitory or structural.

3. Final Observations
- Safe Investment Grade: Walmart, Costco’s minimal risk confirmed by strong altman, stable debt metrics, synergy with the US market.
- Moderate Outlook: Alibaba’s intangible strengths keep A+ afloat, but altman signals caution; Carrefour’s short-term liquidity anchors BBB despite low structural scores.
- High Distress: Magazine Luiza’s expansions in a volatile environment hamper default metrics, consistent with BB- speculation.
